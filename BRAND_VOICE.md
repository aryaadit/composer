# Composer Brand Voice

This document defines how Composer talks. It exists because small copy decisions add up — without a north star, the voice drifts across dozens of small choices (error messages, button labels, empty states, tooltips) and eventually the product sounds like it was written by five different people.

Reference this before writing any user-facing copy.

---

## The one-paragraph summary

Composer is **observational, slightly dry, quietly funny**. It treats the reader like a smart friend who lives in NYC, not a customer. Editorial tone on big copy (headlines, hero sections); conversational tone on utility copy (buttons, errors, empty states). The design is elegant and composed; the words are alive and a little irreverent. Together they feel like a thoughtful friend with good taste who also makes you laugh.

---

## Voice principles

### 1. Observational, not performative

Good copy notices something true. Bad copy tries to be funny.

- ✅ "For group chats"
- ✅ "For random Tuesdays"
- ✅ "For when you're out of ideas"
- ❌ "Level up your night!"
- ❌ "The ultimate NYC planner"
- ❌ "Tired of the same old date night?"

If a line sounds like an ad, cut it. If it sounds like something a friend would text, keep it.

### 2. The irreverence lives in the copy, not the design

Composer's visual identity is editorial — Didot-style serif headlines, cream-and-maroon palette, composed whitespace. The words bring the energy; the design stays elegant.

Never:
- Add emojis to UI chrome (buttons, nav, status badges). Use them sparingly in conversational copy only, and only when they carry meaning.
- Use exclamation points casually. Reserve for genuine celebration moments ("Itinerary saved!" is fine; "Welcome!" is lazy).
- Add goofy illustrations, comic-book fonts, or bright accent colors that fight the editorial aesthetic.
- Use all-caps for emphasis. Bold is sufficient.

### 3. Meet the reader where they live

Composer's users are NYC residents in their 20s–30s. They text in lowercase. They know what a group chat is. They don't need to be explained things like "reservation platform" or "itinerary."

- Use contractions. ("You're" not "you are")
- Say "night out" not "social outing."
- Reference real life, not marketing abstractions. ("Your ex's birthday" not "a special occasion")

### 4. Utility copy is brief and human

Buttons, errors, loading states — these are where voice most often dies. Default phrasing is corporate and forgettable. Fight for the better version when it's cheap to do.

| Default | Composer |
|---|---|
| Something went wrong | Resy's having a moment. Try again? |
| Loading... | Composing... |
| No results found | Nothing here yet |
| Itinerary saved | Plan locked in |
| Please enter a valid phone number | That doesn't look like a phone number |
| Are you sure you want to delete? | This one's gone for good. Sure? |

Shorter is usually better. Specific is always better. "Resy's having a moment" beats "Something went wrong" because it names the problem.

### 5. Avoid the copywriter clichés

Words and phrases that should be banned:
- "Elevated" / "Elevate your..." — corporate-speak
- "Journey" — applied to anything that isn't literal travel
- "Curated" — everything is curated now, word has lost meaning
- "Unlock" — unless literally unlocking something
- "Discover" — feels like a 2015 travel app
- "Perfect" as an adjective on user-facing copy ("Find your perfect night") — aggressive
- "Unleash" — just no
- "Experience" as a noun when it means "event"

Words that are fine to use but worth pausing on:
- "Vibe" — overused in food/drink discovery, but still feels right for Composer's audience. Use but don't overuse.
- "Night" / "evening" / "plans" — natural for this product.

### 6. Editorial tone for big copy; conversational tone for utility copy

**Editorial** = marketing pages, headlines, hero sections, onboarding copy.
- Sparse, deliberate, confident.
- One strong statement better than three soft ones.
- The Didot headline does a lot of the work. Let it.

**Conversational** = errors, tooltips, confirmations, empty states, buttons.
- Direct, friendly, slightly informal.
- Short sentences. Sentence case, not Title Case, except for brand/proper nouns.
- Don't over-explain.

## The smell test

When writing copy, ask:

1. Would a friend text me this? Or does it sound like a push notification from a brand?
2. Is this saying something true, or just trying to sound cool?
3. Am I using a word I'd never say out loud? (journey, elevate, curated)
4. Can I cut half the words?
5. Does it fit on the screen on mobile?

If the copy fails any of these, it's not ready.

---

## When in doubt, err toward less

The elegant design gives us permission to say less. Empty space is fine. A quiet headline is confident. A one-word button ("Save") is usually better than a three-word button ("Save this plan"). Trust the user.
